# Starship SN2 — The "Thrust Puck" That Saved the Program

**Starship SN2** was not a full-scale prototype like SN1, but a smaller-scale test tank designed with a single critical objective: to validate the new **"thrust puck"** (thrust disc) design following the catastrophic failure of SN1 just one week earlier.

## Context and Urgency

After the explosion of SN1 on February 28, 2020, Elon Musk and the SpaceX engineering team identified that the failure was due to a design and welding error in the structure that supports the pressure of the Raptor engines. In an unprecedented display of agility, SpaceX decided to cancel the immediate flight prototype and build SN2 as a test article dedicated exclusively to pressurization.

## Features of SN2

Unlike its predecessors, SN2 essentially consisted of a single propellant tank with a reinforced lower dome section and thrust puck. It had no upper sections, no flaps, and no nosecone. It was a "sacrificial part" designed to be pushed to the limit.

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | ~10 m (optimized tank section) |
| Diameter | 9 m |
| Purpose | Structural validation of the Thrust Puck |
| Result | Total success |

## The Definitive Test — March 8, 2020

On March 8, 2020, SN2 underwent an intense cryogenic pressure test. While the world watched with skepticism following the SN1 failure, SN2 withstood pressures exceeding **8.5 bar**, the threshold considered necessary for safe orbital flight.

The test was not only successful but proved that the new welding design and thrust puck geometry were capable of handling the structural load of the powerful Raptor engines.

## Historical Importance

The success of SN2 was the "green light" for SpaceX to continue with the manufacturing of **SN3** and **SN4**. Without the rapid validation provided by this test tank, the Starship program would have faced months of design redesign delays.

SN2 demonstrated the validity of SpaceX's iterative method: fail on a large part (SN1), test the solution on a specific small part (SN2), and immediately apply the lesson to the next flight vehicle.

## Final Status

After successfully completing its mission, SN2 was retired to make way for the assembly of SN3. Its success paved the way for the iconic prototypes that would perform the first 150-meter hops and high-altitude flights.
