# Starship SN10 — The First Intact Landing

**Starship SN10** wrote a golden page in SpaceX's history by becoming the first full-scale prototype to complete a high-altitude flight and land vertically at the Boca Chica pad. Although the vehicle fell victim to an explosion minutes after landing, the mission fully validated the Starship system's operational takeoff and recovery concept.

## Mission Summary

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | ~50 m |
| Diameter | 9 m |
| Propulsion | 3 Raptor Engines |
| Milestone | First successful landing after 10 km flight (March 3, 2021) |
| Outcome | Landing success; Subsequent RUD (8 min later) |

SN10 incorporated the lessons learned from its predecessors, using a new three-engine reignition strategy for landing, which provided the necessary redundancy for a successful flip maneuver.

## The Historic March 3, 2021 Flight

The 6-minute and 29-second flight followed the standard profile:
1.  **Ascent:** It climbed smoothly to an altitude of **10 km**, shutting down its Raptor engines sequentially.
2.  **Descent Maneuver:** It performed the horizontal descent controlled by flaps.
3.  **Landing Flip & Burn:** Upon approaching the ground, SN10 ignited all three engines, successfully completed the flip to vertical, and then shut down two of them to perform the final descent with a single engine.
4.  **Touchdown:** For the first time, a Starship touched down softly and stood upright on its landing legs.

## The Post-Landing Explosion

Approximately eight minutes after a successful landing, a large methane leak (likely caused by the stress of a slightly "hard" landing that damaged the legs and base) triggered a massive explosion that threw SN10 into the air before it was destroyed.

Despite this outcome, SN10 proved that landing a ship of this size was possible, paving the way for **SN15** to achieve a perfect and safe landing months later.

## Final Status
SN10 fulfilled its primary objective: to land. Its data was used to redesign the landing leg system and improve header tank pressure control during final braking.
