# Starship SN24 — Pioneer of the First Orbital Flight

**Starship SN24** (Ship 24) will go down in history as the first Starship upper-stage prototype to attempt to reach space in an integrated flight with the Super Heavy booster (Booster 7). Its launch marked the beginning of a new era for SpaceX's program and for the first time tested the full system.

## The Path to the First Integrated Flight (IFT-1)

After months of preparation at Boca Chica (Starbase), SN24 was integrated with Booster 7 on the Orbital Launch Pad (OLP). The mission's primary objective was not the recovery of the vehicle but the collection of data on structural integrity, flight dynamics, and the functionality of onboard systems.

## Technical Features

SN24 incorporated massive improvements, such as nearly full coverage of thermal protection tiles and the new Raptor 2 engines, optimized for higher thrust and reliability.

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | 50 m |
| Diameter | 9 m |
| Propulsion | 6 Raptor 2 Engines (3 Sea-level, 3 Vacuum) |
| Platform | OLP Starbase (Boca Chica) |
| Milestone | First integrated flight (April 20, 2023) |

## The Historic Flight of April 20, 2023

In a spectacular liftoff, the Starship system (SN24 + B7) successfully rose above the pad. During approximately 4 minutes of flight, the vehicle faced extreme conditions:
1. Successful flight through the point of maximum dynamic pressure (**Max-Q**).
2. Continued ascent despite the loss of several engines on the booster.
3. An issue with the separation system caused the stack to spin on its axis.
4. Finally, the Flight Termination System (FTS) was activated to safely destroy the vehicle over the Gulf of Mexico.

## Historical Significance

Although orbit was not reached, SN24 demonstrated that the Starbase infrastructure could launch the largest and most powerful rocket ever built. It was the definitive testbed for the thermal protection system and the new Raptor 2 engines.

## Final Status

SN24 was voluntarily destroyed by the FTS on April 20, 2023. The data collected during its brief but intense flight was crucial for redesigning the launch pad and improving the reliability of subsequent versions of the rocket.
