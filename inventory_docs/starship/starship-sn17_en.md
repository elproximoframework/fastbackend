# Starship SN17, SN18, and SN19 — The Transition Toward Orbit

In SpaceX's dynamic development process at Starbase, Texas, prototype numbering doesn't always guarantee that each one will be completed or flown. Models **SN17**, **SN18**, and **SN19** were victims of the Starship program's rapid pace of innovation.

## The End of a Generation

| Prototype | Final Status | Context |
|---|---|---|
| SN17 | Scrapped (June 2021) | Parts repurposed for other prototypes |
| SN18 | Cancelled | Never fully assembled |
| SN19 | Cancelled | Direct jump to orbital architecture (Ship 20) |

As the program progressed, SpaceX realized that suborbital prototypes (designed for 10 km flights) had already served their purpose with the total success of SN15.

## Why Were They Cancelled?

The cancellation of this series of ships was due to several fundamental technical factors:
1.  **Technological Obsolescence:** These models were based on the suborbital architecture. By the summer of 2021, the engineering team had already finalized the design of the first spacecraft capable of reaching orbit: **Ship 20**.
2.  **Heat Shield:** Ship 20 required a hull design capable of supporting thousands of ceramic thermal protection tiles. The designs for SN17-19 were not optimized for this delicate and massive protection system.
3.  **Engine Configuration:** While previous SN models only carried 3 Raptor engines (sea-level), orbital ships required 6 engines (3 sea-level and 3 vacuum-optimized, RVac).

This transition period demonstrated SpaceX's ability to pivot rapidly: instead of manufacturing hardware they knew would be outdated in weeks, they chose to scrap existing material and focus the company's entire bandwidth on the ambitious leap into outer space.
