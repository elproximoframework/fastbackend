# Starship SN23 — The Transition Prototype

**Starship SN23** (Ship 23) was an upper-stage Starship prototype built during a phase of rapid evolution in SpaceX's launch system design. Although it never performed a flight, its construction and subsequent retirement provided valuable data for optimizing manufacturing processes and the transition toward the first integrated flight.

## Context and Development

Built in late 2021 and early 2022, SN23 was part of the group of prototypes (SN20-SN23) designed to test thermal protection and integration with the Super Heavy booster. However, due to rapid advancements in the design of Ship 24, SpaceX decided to prioritize the latter for the first orbital flight attempt (IFT-1), leaving SN23 as a ground test article.

## Features of SN23

SN23 featured structural improvements over the SN15/SN16 series prototypes, including a more refined engine configuration and increased coverage of thermal protection system (TPS) tiles.

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | 50 m |
| Diameter | 9 m |
| Propulsion | 6 Raptor Engines (3 Sea-level, 3 Vacuum) |
| Purpose | Structural validation and transition to orbital flights |
| Result | Retired without flight |

## Testing and Final Fate

Throughout 2022, SN23 underwent various pressure and cryogenic tests at the Starbase facilities. Despite its advanced state of construction, SpaceX determined that Ship 24 and Ship 25 incorporated critical modifications (such as the switch to Raptor 2 engines) that made SN23 technologically obsolete for orbital flight objectives.

## Historical Importance

SN23 represents SpaceX's "rapid discard" philosophy: if an improved design is ready, it is more efficient to retire the previous model than to attempt to adapt it. Its existence allowed for the validation of assembly techniques that were fundamental to the success of Ship 24's assembly.

## Final Status

After being retired from the active production line, SN23 was scrapped in 2022. Its components and lessons learned were integrated directly into the manufacturing of the vehicles that would ultimately perform the program's first integrated flights.
