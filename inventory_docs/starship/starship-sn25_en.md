# Starship SN25 — The Technological Leap

**Starship SN25** (Ship 25) was the second Starship upper-stage prototype to perform an integrated flight (IFT-2). This vehicle introduced critical improvements to the system architecture, most notably being the first to successfully demonstrate the **hot-staging** separation technique, a fundamental milestone for increasing the system's payload capacity.

## Improvements and Unique Features

Unlike SN24, SN25 incorporated thousands of small improvements based on lessons from the first flight. The most visible was the hot-staging ring—a perforated steel structure designed to allow Ship 25's engines to ignite while still attached to the booster.

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | 50 m |
| Diameter | 9 m |
| Propulsion | 6 Raptor 2 Engines (Electronic TVC) |
| Innovation | Hot-staging separation ring |
| Milestone | First successful stage separation (November 18, 2023) |

SN25 also pioneered the use of electric actuators for thrust vector control (TVC) of its Raptor engines, eliminating the need for complex hydraulic systems.

## The Flight of November 18, 2023

The launch from Starbase was flawless. During the ascent:
1. All 33 engines of Booster 9 functioned correctly throughout the initial phase.
2. At approximately 2 minutes and 40 seconds, Ship 25 ignited its engines and separated perfectly from the booster (**Hot-staging**).
3. The ship continued its ascent toward space, reaching an altitude of nearly 150 km and a speed of almost 24,000 km/h.
4. Shortly before the scheduled engine cutoff, telemetry was lost, and the Flight Termination System (FTS) activated autonomously.

## Historical Significance

SN25 proved that Starship was capable of dynamic stage separation and that its steel design could withstand the extreme heat generated during hot-staging. it was the program's first vehicle to reach the boundaries of space in a controlled manner.

## Final Status

SN25 was destroyed over the Atlantic Ocean during the final phase of its ascent on November 18, 2023. Its debris and flight data laid the groundwork for the improvements that allowed the next prototype, Ship 28, to complete an even more successful flight.
