# Starship SN9 — Refining the Descent

**Starship SN9** was SpaceX's second high-altitude prototype. Following the partial success of SN8, SN9 sought to perfect complex aerodynamic maneuvers and the landing system. Although the mission provided invaluable data on vehicle stability during descent, a technical failure during the critical final flip maneuver prevented a successful landing.

## Mission Profile

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | ~50 m |
| Diameter | 9 m |
| Propulsion | 3 Raptor Engines |
| Milestone | 10 km Flight (February 2, 2021) |
| Outcome | RUD on landing due to engine failure |

SN9 demonstrated exceptional aerodynamic stability during its freefall, validating the independent control of its four forward and aft flaps.

## The February 2, 2021 Flight

SN9 lifted off from the Boca Chica pad and reached an altitude of **10 km**. After shutting down its engines and transitioning to horizontal flight, the ship descended in a controlled manner.

However, during the reorientation maneuver (landing flip):
1.  **Reignition Failure:** One of the two Raptor engines selected for landing failed to ignite correctly.
2.  **Loss of Control:** Without the symmetrical thrust needed to straighten the ship, SN9 could not complete the flip to vertical.
3.  **Impact:** The vehicle hit the landing pad at a tilted angle and exploded instantly.

## Lessons Learned

The loss of SN9 led Elon Musk and his engineering team to rethink the landing strategy. The primary conclusion was the need for greater redundancy: from then on, SpaceX decided to attempt to ignite all three engines during the final flip, then shut down the ones that were not necessary, always ensuring at least two were working correctly for the final touchdown.

This strategic shift would be fundamental to the partial success of the next prototype, **SN10**.
