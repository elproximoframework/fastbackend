# Starship SN4 — The First Cryogenic Survivor

**Starship SN4** marked a critical turning point in SpaceX's development program. Following the structural failures of the SN1 and SN3 prototypes, SN4 became the first full-scale prototype to survive rigorous cryogenic pressure testing, proving the maturity of the company's stainless steel design and welding techniques.

## The Path to Structural Success

Unlike its predecessors, which failed during pressurization with liquid nitrogen, SN4 withstood multiple cycles of cryogenic testing in April and May 2020. On April 26, 2020, it reached **4.9 bar**, and subsequently passed even more intense tests, finally validating the tank architecture for live-fire testing.

## Technical Milestones and Engine Testing

SN4 was the first prototype to integrate a functional Raptor engine (SN18 and later SN20) and successfully perform a static fire.

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | ~30 m (Full tank section) |
| Diameter | 9 m |
| Engines | 1 Raptor (SN18 / SN20) |
| Static Fires | 5 Successful |
| Final Status | Destroyed by GSE failure |

In total, the vehicle completed **five static fires**, the most for any prototype up to that time. These successes allowed SpaceX to obtain an FAA license for a 150-meter test flight.

## The May 29, 2020 Incident

On May 29, 2020, just one minute after completing its fifth successful static fire, a massive methane leak caused by a failure in the ground support equipment's (GSE) quick-disconnect system triggered a catastrophic explosion that completely destroyed the vehicle.

Despite its loss, SN4 is remembered as the prototype that "saved" the current design, proving that stainless steel was the right material and that the system could handle the stress of Raptor engine operations.

## Legacy

The success of SN4's pressure tests allowed subsequent prototypes, such as SN5 and SN6, to focus directly on flight milestones (hops), drastically accelerating the Starship program's development timeline.
