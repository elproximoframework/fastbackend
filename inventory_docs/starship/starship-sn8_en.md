# Starship SN8 — The Era of High-Altitude Flights

**Starship SN8** marked the beginning of the most ambitious phase of SpaceX's development program. It was the first prototype to incorporate a functional nose cone, forward and aft flaps for aerodynamic control, and the first to be powered by three Raptor engines. Its mission was to validate the "belly flop" maneuver (horizontal descent) and the final landing flip.

## Revolutionary Features

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | ~50 m (Complete ship with nose cone) |
| Diameter | 9 m |
| Propulsion | 3 Raptor Engines (SN30, SN36, SN42) |
| Milestone | 12.5 km Flight (December 9, 2020) |
| Outcome | RUD on landing (95% Success) |

Unlike the "flying silos" SN5 and SN6, SN8 looked like a complete spacecraft. It was the pioneer in using aerodynamic control surfaces to navigate through the atmosphere during descent.

## The Historic 12.5 km Flight

On December 9, 2020, SN8 performed a 6-minute and 42-second mission that amazed the world:
1.  **Ascent:** It used its three Raptor engines to climb to 12.5 km, shutting them down in sequence to conserve propellant.
2.  **Belly Flop:** It tilted horizontally, using its flaps to maintain stability while falling freely toward Earth.
3.  **Landing Flip:** Near the ground, the engines reignited to flip the ship from horizontal to vertical.
4.  **Landing:** Due to low pressure in the fuel header tank, the engines lacked sufficient flow, causing oxygen-rich combustion (green flame) and a high-speed impact.

## Legacy of SN8

Although the vehicle was lost in a spectacular RUD (Rapid Unscheduled Disassembly), Elon Musk called the test a total success. SN8 proved that the Starship system's aerodynamics worked and that the "belly flop" was a viable reentry technique. The data obtained was fundamental in correcting header tank pressure issues in later versions.
