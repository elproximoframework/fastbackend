# Starship SN3

## Overview

Starship SN3 was the third full-scale prototype of SpaceX's Starship vehicle, built and tested at the Boca Chica (Starbase) facility in Texas during the spring of 2020. Although it never flew, its destruction during a cryogenic pressure test provided critical data that accelerated the development of subsequent prototypes. Paradoxically, its collapse was not the result of a design failure but of a human error in test configuration, which allowed SpaceX to continue without modifying the base structure of the next vehicle, SN4.

## History

### Context and Manufacturing (March 2020)

In early 2020, SpaceX was in the middle of an accelerated Starship prototype iteration campaign. SN3's two direct predecessors had both been destroyed during pressure tests: the Mk.1 prototype collapsed in November 2019, and SN1 suffered a catastrophic failure on February 28, 2020. SN2, stripped down to just its tank section, was used exclusively as a thrust puck test article and passed its tests successfully on March 8.

SN3 was the first prototype assembled in the new Boca Chica assembly building, taking advantage of expanded facilities and improved manufacturing techniques compared to its predecessors. It incorporated lessons learned from all previous test articles. Elon Musk shared images of the stacking process prior to its rollout to the launch pad.

### Rollout and Pad Preparations (March 29, 2020)

On March 29, SN3 was rolled out to the test area and lifted onto the launch mount. Among its notable features was a new internal fold-out landing leg design, though these were shorter than those planned for SN4. The vehicle had no nosecone and no aerodynamic fins, as it was intended solely for ground tests and, at best, a low-altitude hop of around 150 meters.

The planned test sequence was as follows:
1. Ambient-temperature pressure test with gaseous nitrogen.
2. Cryogenic pressure test with liquid nitrogen.
3. Static fire with Raptor engines.
4. Low-altitude hop flight to ~150 meters.

### Ambient Pressure Test Passed (April 1, 2020)

On the night of April 1, SN3 successfully passed the first ambient-temperature pressure test. Elon Musk confirmed the result on Twitter: *"SN3 passed ambient temperature pressure test last night, now doing cryogenic."* The team then moved forward with preparations for the cryogenic test.

### Collapse During Cryogenic Test (April 3, 2020)

In the early hours of April 3, at approximately 2:00 a.m. local time (CDT), SN3 collapsed dramatically while undergoing its cryogenic pressure test. The vehicle was being filled with liquid nitrogen at cryogenic temperatures and flight pressures when a commanding error in the test configuration resulted in the inadvertent depressurization of the LOX (liquid oxygen) tank in the aft section of the vehicle. Without the internal pressure needed to support the structure, the LOX tank buckled under the weight of the full methane tank above it. The vehicle crumpled approximately halfway up its height and collapsed onto the test stand.

Cameras located on Boca Chica Beach, operated by local residents and NASASpaceFlight, captured the full moment of collapse, including the white vapor clouds that accompanied the vehicle's implosion.

Elon Musk, referring to early hypotheses, tweeted: *"We will see what data review says in the morning, but this may have been a test configuration mistake."* Subsequent data review confirmed this assessment: there was no material failure or structural design flaw in the vehicle itself.

### Aftermath and Parts Recovery

Since the collapse was the result of an operational error rather than a design defect, SpaceX determined that SN4 required no structural modifications. Undamaged components from SN3 were recovered and repurposed for SN4's construction, minimizing material waste and maintaining production cadence.

The FAA had issued a NOTAM for the area covering April 6–8, the dates originally planned for the static fire and hop test, but those objectives were carried over entirely to SN4's test campaign.

## Innovations

Although SN3 did not complete its test program, it introduced or validated several important advances in the Starship development process:

- **New Assembly Building:** SN3 was the first prototype assembled in the new Boca Chica manufacturing building, offering greater space and tooling than previous facilities, laying the groundwork for serial production.

- **Internal Fold-Out Landing Leg Design:** SN3 was the first vehicle to feature a new internally mounted fold-out landing leg system. While shorter than the final design, this architecture anticipated the solution adopted in subsequent vehicles.

- **Improved Stacking Process:** The steel ring stacking techniques refined during SN3's construction established the standard procedure that SpaceX would replicate across SN4, SN5, and later prototypes.

- **Critical Operational Lesson:** SN3's collapse highlighted the critical importance of differential tank pressurization procedures during ground testing, a lesson that was directly integrated into the test protocols for all subsequent vehicles.

- **Modular Production Validation:** The reuse of SN3 components in SN4 demonstrated the viability of the modular, high-cadence manufacturing approach that SpaceX intended to scale massively, and proved that even a failed test vehicle could contribute materially to the program.
