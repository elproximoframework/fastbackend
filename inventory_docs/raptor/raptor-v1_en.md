# Raptor 1 — The Engine That Paved the Way

**Type:** Liquid methane / LOX rocket engine  
**Version:** Raptor 1 (Block 1)  
**Cycle:** Full-flow staged combustion (FFSC)  
**Test facility:** McGregor, Texas  
**First ignition:** September 26, 2017  
**Status:** Retired — superseded by Raptor 2 (2022)  

---

## Technical Specifications

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Sea-level thrust | ~185 tf (1,813 kN) |
| Sea-level specific impulse (ISP) | ~330 s |

| Engine dry mass | ~2,080 kg |
| Fuel | CH₄ — liquid methane |
| Oxidizer | LOX — liquid oxygen |
| Thermodynamic cycle | Full-flow staged combustion (FFSC) |
| Throttle range | 40–100% |

## History

### Origin and Development
The Raptor engine program began around 2012, when SpaceX started planning a methane/LOX propellant engine capable of powering future missions to Mars. Methane was chosen for several key reasons: it can be synthesized on Mars from atmospheric CO₂ and water ice (Sabatier process), it has a superior energy density compared to RP-1 kerosene for long-range missions, and it is compatible with full engine reusability.

The **full-flow staged combustion (FFSC) cycle** was the most technically ambitious design choice. In this cycle, both the fuel and oxidizer pass completely through their respective pre-burners before entering the main combustion chamber, maximizing efficiency and achieving extraordinarily high chamber pressures. Prior to Raptor, no operational rocket engine had successfully demonstrated this cycle in a stable, repeatable manner.

On September 26, 2017, SpaceX conducted the first ignition of a test Raptor engine at its McGregor, Texas facility. In February 2019, Raptor SN1 completed the first full-duration ignition test at the target production engine specifications.

### Starship Flights
Raptor 1 powered every generation of Starship during the development phase in Boca Chica through 2022:

- **SN5 (August 2020):** The first 150m hop of a full-scale Starship, powered by a single Raptor 1.
- **SN6 (September 2020):** Repeated the 150m hop, validating single-engine flight repeatability.
- **SN8 (December 2020):** First high-altitude flight (12.5 km) with three Raptor 1 engines; first "belly flop" maneuver. Hard landing.
- **SN9 & SN10:** Further high-altitude test flights with progressive Raptor improvements.
- **SN15 (May 2021):** Historic milestone — first successful landing of a full Starship. Raptor 1 demonstrated reliability and re-ignition capability for propulsive landing.

### Phased Retirement
In early 2022, SpaceX began introducing the **Raptor 2** — a radically simplified engine, 20 tf more powerful (up to 230 tf), and significantly lighter (1,720 kg). Raptor 1 production gradually wound down, though some engines continued in use during the transition period. IFT-1 (April 2023) flew entirely powered by Raptor 2 engines.

## Innovations

- **First operational FFSC engine:** Raptor 1 was the first rocket engine using the full-flow staged combustion cycle to demonstrate repeatable flight — a landmark achievement in propulsion engineering.
- **Methane era pioneer:** It opened the door for high-efficiency methane/LOX engines, a key technology for 21st-century planetary missions.
- **In-flight re-ignition:** The first SpaceX engine designed for multiple in-flight re-ignitions, essential for Starship's propulsive landing capability.
- **"Christmas tree" plumbing:** The most visually distinctive feature of Raptor 1 was its complex network of external piping and sensors — a hallmark of its development-platform nature. Raptor 2 would eliminate much of this external plumbing by internalizing systems.
