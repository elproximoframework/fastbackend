# Super Heavy BN1 — First Super Booster Prototype

**Type:** Manufacturing Pathfinder  
**Serial Number:** BN1  
**Location:** Starbase, Boca Chica, Texas  
**Assembly Completed:** March 17, 2021  
**Scrapped:** March 30, 2021  
**Status:** Scrapped — never flew

## History

Super Heavy BN1 (Booster 1) is the first prototype built for the Super Heavy booster rocket within SpaceX's Starship program. Unlike the Starship SN-series vehicles that flew at Boca Chica, BN1 was never intended to launch: it was designed from the outset as a **manufacturing pathfinder** — a full-scale test article to validate assembly processes before building the first flight-capable boosters.

Construction of BN1 began in December 2020 at the Starbase facility. The stacking of 36 stainless steel rings was carried out inside the High Bay (the large assembly building), starting in February 2021. On March 17, 2021, the full stack of BN1 was completed, reaching an approximate height of 67 meters — making it the tallest stacked stainless steel structure in the program at that time.

Just two weeks after completion, SpaceX founder Elon Musk publicly confirmed on Twitter that BN1 would not fly and would be scrapped. SpaceX chose to incorporate the manufacturing lessons from BN1 directly into the design of Booster 2 (BN2), which would include significant improvements. Dismantling began around March 30, 2021.

Although its operational lifespan was extremely short, BN1 was fundamental in defining the manufacturing procedures for the Super Heavy program, validating the feasibility of building and stacking a rocket of this scale with the speed and cost consistent with SpaceX's iterative engineering philosophy.

---

## Innovations and Legacy

- **First-of-kind stacking:** BN1 was the first attempt to stack a 9-meter-diameter rocket manufactured from 304L stainless steel at this scale, establishing the foundational stacking procedures used in the High Bay.
- **Thrust donut design:** BN1 incorporated a thrust section configured for only 4 Raptor 1 engines, enabling structural study of the "donut" ring (the lower ring where engines are mounted) before scaling to 29 or 33 engines in subsequent boosters.
- **Stainless steel validation:** The welding process, ring qualification, and structural integrity of full-scale stainless steel rings were validated in hardware for the first time on BN1.
- **Iterative engineering:** Its rapid obsolescence and scrapping exemplified SpaceX's manufacturing model: build fast, learn fast, scrap if necessary, and keep improving.
