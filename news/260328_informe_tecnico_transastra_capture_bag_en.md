# Technical Report: TransAstra — Asteroid Mining with Inflatable Bags (Capture Bag)
**Date:** March 2026 | **Classification:** Technical disclosure | **Sources:** NASA CCRPP, ISS National Lab, SpaceNews, CNN, Gizmodo

---

## 1. Context: Why Mine Asteroids

C-type and S-type Near-Earth Asteroids (NEAs) contain water, metals, and radiation shielding materials in concentrations impossible to match by transporting them from Earth. Moving a ton of material from Earth's surface to low Earth orbit costs between $1,000 and $2,000/kg even with today's cheapest launchers. In contrast, an asteroid in a solar orbit very similar to Earth's can be reached with **less delta-v than is necessary to reach the Moon**.

TransAstra, a Los Angeles-based aerospace startup founded in 2015 by engineer and former Caltech professor **Joel Sercel**, has developed a complete technological chain to address this problem by structuring the challenge into four stages: **detect, capture, move, and process**.

---

## 2. Stage 1: Detection — Sutter Telescope Network

Before capturing an asteroid, the right candidate must be found. TransAstra operates its own network of survey telescopes named **Sutter** (referencing Sutter's Mill, where gold was discovered in California in 1848):

- **Current locations:** Arizona, California, and Australia (fourth location planned in Spain)
- **Objective:** To identify asteroids from the *Arjuna* family (nearly circular orbits very similar to Earth's), between 5 and 20 meters in diameter
- **Expected catalog:** The **Vera C. Rubin** observatory in Chile will enable the discovery of ~260 new objects up to 20 meters in the coming years
- **Selection criteria:** Asteroids in low relative energy orbits (Slowly Drifting Objects, SDO), reachable with minimal delta-v maneuvers

## 3. Stage 2: Capture — Capture Bag System

### System Description

The **Capture Bag** is a modular inflatable bag designed to envelop objects of any shape, size, and rotation state (including tumbling objects, the industry's greatest technological challenge). Unlike robotic arms or harpoons, the system does not require the target to have regular geometry or defined gripping surfaces.

### Materials and Construction

| Component | Material |
|---|---|
| Outer structure | Kevlar and aluminum (standard aerospace applications) |
| Sealing | Hermetic design certified for vacuum |
| Closing mechanism | Active cinching (reversible closing and opening multiple times) |
| Deployment system | Autonomous inflation from the carrier vehicle |

### Size Scaling

| Size | Diameter | Maximum Capacity | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Terrestrial prototype | ~30 cm | Vacuum chamber validation | ✅ Completed |
| Small (ISS) | **1 meter** | Microgravity demonstration | ✅ Completed (Oct. 2025) |
| Large (JPL) | **10 meters** | ~100 t asteroids / massive orbital debris | 🔄 In development (2026) |
| Final operational | **32 feet (9.75 m)** | Target asteroid for New Moon mission | 🎯 Expected 2027–28 |

The 10-meter bag is large enough to contain a **10,000-ton** asteroid, equivalent in volume to a small building.

---

## 4. ISS Demonstration — October 2, 2025

TransAstra successfully completed a demonstration of the Capture Bag technology on the ISS on October 2, 2025. The operational details of the test were as follows:

- **Test location:** Bishop Airlock on Voyager Space's commercial module, exterior of the ISS
- **Conditions:** Real vacuum and microgravity (not simulated on the ground)
- **Protocol:** Astronauts deployed and retracted the 1-meter bag multiple times, validating the opening and closing cycles under operational conditions
- **Result:** Complete success. CEO Sercel described the milestone as the first operation in space of its inflatable technology, laying the groundwork for orbital debris remediation and asteroid capture.
- **Hardware-to-flight development time:** 7 months from design to flight hardware delivery

---

## 5. Stage 3: Transfer — Omnivore Solar Thermal Propulsion Engine

Once captured, the asteroid must be moved to a stable orbit near the Earth-Moon system. To this end, TransAstra is developing the **Omnivore** thruster:

- **Operating principle:** Solar Thermal Propulsion (STP); concentrates sunlight using mirrors to heat and expel propellant (water), generating high-efficiency thrust
- **Key advantage:** Water can be extracted directly from the captured asteroid itself (if it is C-type), creating a self-sustaining propellant cycle
- **Estimated specific impulse:** 800–1,000 s (compared to ~450 s for conventional chemical engines)
- **Secondary application:** In-orbit electrical power generation for processing operations

---

## 6. Stage 4: Processing — New Moon Mission

TransAstra's target operational mission is named **New Moon**:

- **Objective:** To capture a ~100-metric-ton asteroid from the Arjuna family
- **Target orbit:** Stable orbit in the Earth-Moon system (L4 or L5 Lagrange point, or DRO)
- **Resources to extract:** Water (splittable into H₂ and O₂ for propellant), ferrous metals, rare earth elements, and inert shielding material
- **Estimated cost:** A few hundred million dollars (according to CEO estimate)
- **Long-term vision:** Accumulate hundreds of asteroids during the 2030s to constitute a million tons of material in orbit; transform them into robotic research and industrial processing bases

## 8. Dual Use: Space Junk

The Capture Bag is not exclusively a mining tool. The system is designed to capture objects of various shapes and sizes, including those rotating uncontrollably, the greatest technical challenge of orbital debris removal. Moving debris to an orbital reprocessing facility, rather than direct deorbiting, would cost six times less, consume 80% less propellant, and clear a given orbit 40% faster. The combined market for debris cleanup and asteroid mining is estimated at more than **$1 billion/year by 2030**.
