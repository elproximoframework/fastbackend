# Artemis 2 Mission: Orion Approaches the Moon in Humanity's Farthest Space Journey

The climax of NASA's **Artemis 2** mission to the Moon will arrive on Monday night into Tuesday (Spanish time) when the Orion capsule flies over the far side of the Moon for three hours. The mission's four astronauts will gaze upon a territory that no human being has seen before, coinciding with the moment they become the people who have traveled farthest in the history of space exploration.

## A Journey of Milestones and Records

Before that, they must travel the 390,000 kilometers separating Earth from the Moon in a journey that will last four days. With the **translunar injection** successfully completed — a 5-minute and 55-second ignition — Orion's trajectory is now controlled by lunar gravity.

The most notable record will come when the spacecraft reaches its maximum distance from Earth: **407,000 kilometers**. The crew will thus surpass by 6,700 kilometers the record held by the Apollo 13 mission since 1970.

## Comfort and Technology in Deep Space

Unlike the Apollo program, the Orion capsule offers significant improvements in habitability for the four astronauts (three Americans and one Canadian):

- **Living Space:** 8.9 cubic meters compared to the 6.2 of the Apollo capsules.
- **Nutrition:** Lyophilized foods perfected after six decades of missions.
- **Privacy:** They have a small toilet isolated from the rest of the capsule.
- **Rest:** A schedule that allows for eight and a half hours of sleep daily.

## Breaking News: Mission Midpoint Surpassed

The four astronauts are already halfway there and continue to approach the satellite. In their daily briefing, they confirmed having spotted the Moon's **Oriental Basin**, one of the most impressive impact structures in the solar system. After rounding the Moon, they will begin the journey back with a figure-eight trajectory, aiming to splash down in the Pacific off the coast of California on Saturday, April 11.
