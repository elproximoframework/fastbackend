# Technical Report: Hubble Accidentally Captures the Disintegration of Comet C/2025 K1 (ATLAS)
**Date:** March 21, 2026 | **Classification:** Technical disclosure | **Sources:** NASA Goddard, ESA/Hubble, STScI, Icarus Journal, ScienceDaily

---

## 1. Context: An Extraordinarily Improbable Accident

On March 21, 2026, NASA published a study in the journal **Icarus** describing one of the most fortuitous astronomical events of the last decade: the Hubble Space Telescope captured the progressive fragmentation of a comet in real-time. The observation was unplanned, and the statistical probability of it occurring while the telescope was pointing exactly at that object at that exact moment was described by the scientific team as *"extraordinarily minuscule"*.

> *"Sometimes the best science happens by accident. This comet was observed because our original target was not visible due to new technical constraints that arose after our observation proposal was granted. We had to find a new target, and just as we observed it, it turned out to break apart, which is the smallest of small probabilities."*
> — **John Noonan**, Co-Investigator, Auburn University (Alabama)

## 2. Comet C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) — Characteristics and Orbit

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Official Designation | C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) |
| Type | Long-period comet |
| Original Diameter (nucleus) | ~8 km (slightly larger than average) |
| Perihelion (closest point to the Sun) | **0.33 AU** — within Mercury's orbit |
| Perihelion Date | **October 8, 2025** |
| Current Distance from Earth | ~400 million km (constellation Pisces) |
| Orbital Destiny | Ejected from the solar system, **no return** |

> **Note:** Should not be confused with **3I/ATLAS**, the interstellar comet detected in 2025. They are completely different objects that share the ATLAS designation because they were discovered by the same early warning system.

## 3. Observation Instruments — Hubble STIS and COS

The fragmentation images were captured by two Hubble instruments operating in complementary modes:

**STIS — Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph:**
- Primary instrument used for sequence imaging
- High spatial resolution CCD mode
- Allowed individual fragments with separate comas to be distinguished, invisible from the ground
- Spectral range: far-ultraviolet to optical (115 – 1,030 nm)

**COS — Cosmic Origins Spectrograph:**
- High-sensitivity ultraviolet spectroscopy instrument
- Used for analyzing the chemical composition of the gas released during fragmentation
- Spectral range: far and near-ultraviolet (115 – 320 nm)

The combination of both instruments allows for both the **structural image** of the fragments and the **chemical profile** of the volatiles sublimated during the breakup.

| November 11, 2025 | Noonan reviews the data and detects 4 comets where there should be 1 |
| February 6, 2026 | Publication of the study in the journal *Icarus* |
| March 21, 2026 | NASA Goddard publishes the official press release and image sequence |

The three images, each 20 seconds long, taken on consecutive days, constitute the **first direct record of a comet in the early hours of its disintegration** in the entire history of space astronomy.

## 5. Fragmentation Mechanics — Scientific Analysis

### Main Mechanism: Post-Perihelion Thermal Stress

Long-period comets like K1, originating from the Oort Cloud, make very few approaches to the Sun throughout their history. Each passage through the inner solar system exposes them for the first time to extreme heating for which they are not structurally "prepared," unlike short-period comets that have been tempered by multiple orbits.

The breakup process develops in several phases:
1. Solar heat sublimates surface volatiles (CO, CO₂, H₂O)
2. Internal vapor pressure exceeds the tensile strength of the porous nucleus
3. The nucleus fractures along pre-existing structural planes of weakness
4. Each fragment develops its own individual coma (envelope of gas and dust)
5. Smaller fragments, with less mass and resistance, continue to subdivide

### The Mystery of the Luminous Delay

The team identified an unresolved anomaly: fragmentation occurred **8 days before** brightness outbursts were observed from the ground. If the breakup exposed fresh ice, why didn't the comet brighten experimentally almost instantly?

The hypotheses proposed in the *Icarus* article are:

- **Surface Dust Hypothesis:** A comet's brightness comes primarily from dust, not ice. Freshly exposed ice first needs to develop a thin layer of dust that can be expelled by sublimation before generating a visible flash.
- **Subsurface Heat Hypothesis:** Heat must accumulate beneath the surface before releasing an expansive dust cloud that raises the apparent magnitude.

Spectroscopic analysis of the emitted gases is still ongoing and may resolve the enigma in the coming months.

## 6. Anomalous Chemical Composition of K1

Preliminary spectroscopic analyses from the ground revealed that comet C/2025 K1 has an **atypical chemical composition**:

- **Heavily depleted in carbon** compared to other long-period comets
- Unusually low levels of carbonaceous molecules such as C₂ and CN
- This peculiarity raised suspicions about its structural fragility even before the Hubble observation

Scientists are investigating whether the absence of certain carbonaceous volatiles made the nucleus **more porous and susceptible to thermal fragmentation**, accelerating the breakup process even before reaching maximum gravitational stress at perihelion.

## 7. Why This Observation is Unique in History

| Feature | Importance |
|---|---|
| First time Hubble captures such an early fragmentation | ~8 h after the start of the breakup |
| Sufficient resolution to separate individual fragments | Earth: only blurry smudges |
| Chronological reconstruction (backtrace) | Allows calculation of the exact moment of breakup |
| Long-period comet (Oort) | More informative about the primal composition of the solar system |
| Publication in *Icarus* (Feb. 2026) | First exhaustive analysis of such an early disintegration |

---

## 8. Implications for the ESA Comet Interceptor Mission

The results from K1 have a direct impact on the planning of the ESA **Comet Interceptor** mission, scheduled for late this decade, which will be the first probe to visit a long-period comet before its perihelion.

> *"Hubble's fortuitous observation of K1 will help us understand why some long-period comets fragment and give us a first look at their interior. These new results will complement the detailed view we will get from Comet Interceptor, in addition to helping astronomers select the mission's target."*
> — **Prof. Colin Snodgrass**, University of Edinburgh, Interdisciplinary Scientist for Comet Interceptor
