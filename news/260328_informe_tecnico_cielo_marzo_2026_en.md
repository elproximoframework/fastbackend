## 1. Context: An Exceptional Month for Observational Astronomy

March 2026 has offered two top-tier naked-eye visual observation events: the **alignment of six planets** in the evening sky (late February–early March) and the **total lunar eclipse** or Blood Moon on March 3. Both are orbital independent of each other, but their temporal proximity makes this period one of the richest for amateur astronomy in recent years.

## 2. The Planetary Parade — Alignment of Six Planets

### 2.1 Celestial Mechanics: Why it Occurs

The planets of the solar system orbit the Sun in the same plane, called the **ecliptic**. From Earth, the planets follow a narrow band of the sky aligned with this plane. A planetary alignment occurs when several planets are situated on the same side of the Sun relative to Earth, thus appearing grouped in a compact region of the sky. They do not form a straight line in three-dimensional space; it is a perspective effect projected onto the celestial vault.

## 3. Total Lunar Eclipse — Blood Moon, March 3, 2026

### 3.1 Eclipse Mechanics

A total lunar eclipse occurs when Earth is positioned exactly between the Sun and the Full Moon, casting its shadow (umbra) onto the lunar disk. The Moon does not turn black: Earth's atmosphere **refracts and filters** sunlight, allowing only red/orange wavelengths to pass through, projecting onto the Moon the combined color of all terrestrial sunrises and sunsets occurring simultaneously. The intensity of the red depends on the content of aerosols, dust, and clouds in the atmosphere at that moment.

### 3.3 Global Visibility

| Region | Visibility |
|---|---|
| Northwest North America, Hawaii, Pacific | ✅ Full eclipse (all phases) |
| East Asia (Japan, Korea, East Australia) | ✅ Full eclipse |
| Rest of North America | ⚠️ Partial totality (Moon sets during totality) |
| Western South America | ⚠️ Only partial phases at dawn |
| Western Europe (Spain, etc.) | ❌ Moon below horizon during the event |
| Africa and Middle East | ❌ Not visible |

> For Spain (mainland), the eclipse was completely invisible: the Moon was below the horizon for the entire duration of the event. The best available images come from the Kitt Peak National Observatory (Arizona) and observers in Australia and Japan.

### 3.4 Singularities of the March 2026 Eclipse

- It is the **last total lunar eclipse until December 31, 2028** (already dubbed the *New Year's Eve Blood Moon*), giving it special educational relevance.
- During totality, the Moon occulted the deep-sky object **NGC 3423** (spiral galaxy), an extremely rare phenomenon given that deep-sky objects are rarely occulted by the Moon during totality from any point on Earth.
- The eclipse fell on the Chinese **Lantern Festival** (Yuánxiāo Jié), the first to coincide with this festival since February 11, 2017.
- It belongs to a **near-tetrad**: three consecutive total eclipses with the fourth (August 28, 2026) being only partial.

## 4. Relationship Between Both Events: Shared Orbital Geometry

Both events are a product of the same fundamental geometry: the Sun-Earth-planets alignment along the ecliptic. The late February planetary alignment and the March 3 eclipse are part of the same 35-day **eclipse season cycle**, which this year also includes the annular solar eclipse of February 17, 2026. This period concentrates three Sun-Earth alignment events in less than three weeks.
