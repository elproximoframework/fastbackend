# Vera C. Rubin Observatory: Revolutionizing Time Domain Astronomy

Astronomy is about to undergo a paradigm shift with the operational debut of the **Vera C. Rubin Observatory**. Located in the high peaks of the Chilean Andes, this instrument is not just another telescope, but a cosmic surveillance machine designed to detect events that change in fractions of a second.

## An Eye That Never Blinks

The Rubin Observatory's strategy is unique: it will image every point in the Southern Hemisphere sky approximately **800 times over a 10-year period**. This repeated observation capability is the foundation of "time domain astronomy," allowing for the identification of objects that appear, disappear, move, or change in brightness.

Currently, the observatory is already issuing about **800,000 alerts per night**, a figure expected to scale to **10 million daily alerts** once operating at full capacity.

## Objectives: From Asteroids to Planet 9

Scientists expect Rubin's data to revolutionize our understanding of the solar system and beyond:

- **Asteroids:** The observatory is estimated to discover **4 million new asteroids**, tripling the count of 1.5 million identified to date.
- **Comets:** It will trace comets and trans-Neptunian objects in the coldest, outermost regions of the solar system.
- **Planet 9:** It is considered the definitive tool to confirm or debunk the existence of the hypothetical "Planet 9" by analyzing the orbits of distant objects.

## A Dataset for History

The dataset generated by the Vera C. Rubin will become one of the most valuable resources for the global scientific community. By systematically documenting temporal variations, the observatory will not only find new planets and minor bodies but also help understand complex astronomical processes such as stellar rotations and the orbital dynamics that define the structure of the universe.
