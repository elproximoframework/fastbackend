# Technical Report: Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope — Pre-flight Testing
**Date:** March 2026 | **Classification:** Technical disclosure | **Sources:** NASA Goddard, Space.com, Wikipedia, Friends of NASA

---

## 1. Context: The Successor to Hubble

The **Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope** (Roman Space Telescope, RST) is NASA's next flagship astrophysics mission, managed from the **Goddard Space Flight Center** in Greenbelt, Maryland. Recommended in 2010 by the National Research Council's Decadal Survey as the top priority mission of the decade, it was officially approved for development in February 2016. It is named after **Nancy Grace Roman**, NASA's first Chief of Astronomy and a key figure in the creation of the space telescope program.

With an estimated total cost of over **$4 billion**, Roman is not just the successor to Hubble in generational terms: it represents a qualitative leap in wide-field observation capability that no previous space telescope has offered.

---

## 2. Observatory Technical Specifications

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Primary Mirror | 2.4 m diameter (identical to Hubble, NRO origin) |
| Field of View (WFI) | **0.28 deg²** — 100 times larger than Hubble |
| Spectral Range | 0.5 – 2.3 µm (optical and near-infrared) |
| Number of WFI Detectors | 18 H4RG detectors of 4 Megapixels = **72 total Megapixels** |
| Angular Resolution | ~0.11 arcsec/pixel |
| Second Instrument | Coronagraph Instrument (CGI) for direct imaging of exoplanets |
| Total Observatory Mass | ~4,200 kg |
| Nominal Lifetime | 5 years (with propellant for 10 years) |
| Operational Orbit | Earth-Sun L2 (1.5 million km from Earth) |
| Launch Vehicle | **SpaceX Falcon Heavy** (~$255M launch contract) |

## 3. Observatory Structure — Two Major Assemblies

NASA has divided Roman into two major assemblies that have undergone independent testing before final integration:

**Inner Assembly (Core):**
- Optical telescope (primary and secondary mirror)
- Instrument carrier
- Scientific instruments: WFI and CGI coronagraph
- Spacecraft bus

**Outer Assembly:**
- Outer barrel assembly (OBA)
- Deployable aperture cover (DAC)
- Flight solar panels (installed in fall 2025)
- Solar array sun shield

<br>

Both assemblies were integrated in **November 2025**, resulting in the complete observatory. Construction was declared finished on **November 25, 2025**.

## 5. The March 2026 Acoustic Test — Technical Data

The acoustic test is the most visually striking and one of the most structurally demanding tests. The protocol followed in the March 2026 test was as follows:

- **Facility:** High-power acoustic chamber at NASA Goddard Space Flight Center
- **Sound Generation:** Six-foot-tall horn projecting sound waves at various frequencies
- **Sound Pressure Level Reached:** **138 dB** — equivalent to the noise of a jet engine at 30 meters
- **Test Duration:** ~1-minute series per frequency, sweeping from 5 to 50 Hz, repeated in multiple configurations
- **Sensorization:** Hundreds of sensors installed on the structure capture the observatory's dynamic response in real-time
- **Purpose:** To validate that optical components, electrical connections, deployable mechanisms, and the overall structure survive the acoustic loads of launch

> *"If you've ever been to a concert with very loud bass, that load you felt was acoustic energy. Now imagine how loud a launch is. Acoustics can generate very high loads on a large structure like Roman."*
> — **Cory Powell**, Roman lead structural analyst at NASA Goddard

Additionally, the observatory was placed on a **shaker table** that reproduced the rocket's low-frequency vibrations in three axes (vertical, lateral, and longitudinal). The tests reached **125% of the forces** the observatory will experience during the actual launch.

---

## 6. Electromagnetic Interference (EMI) Test — January 2026

Prior to the acoustic test, a critical EMI test for scientific performance was completed in January 2026:

- Absorbent panels were installed around the observatory to isolate the electromagnetic field
- All Roman electronics were turned on and the generated signals were measured
- Objective: to ensure that internal electrical noise does not interfere with the detection of very weak infrared signals from distant objects
- Result: the observatory passed with no detected anomalies

## 7. Scientific Objectives

Roman is designed to address three major questions in contemporary astrophysics:

1. **Dark Energy** — By studying the universe's accelerated expansion through Type Ia supernovae and weak gravitational lensing on a wide-sky scale
2. **Exoplanets** — Gravitational microlensing toward the galactic bulb to detect Earth-mass planets; the CGI coronagraph will allow direct imaging of gas giants
3. **Infrastructure Astrophysics** — Deep infrared surveys of galaxies, clusters, and large-scale structures that will serve as a reference map for the entire astronomical community for decades
