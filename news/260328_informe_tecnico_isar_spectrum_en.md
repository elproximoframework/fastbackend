# Technical Report: "Onward and Upward" Mission — Isar Aerospace / Spectrum Rocket
**Date:** March 25–26, 2026 | **Classification:** Technical disclosure | **Sources:** Isar Aerospace, ESA, NASASpaceFlight, Space.com

---

## 1. Context and Strategic Relevance

On March 25, 2026, the German company **Isar Aerospace** attempted the second orbital flight of its **Spectrum** rocket from the **Andøya** spaceport in northern Norway. Success would have marked a historic milestone: the **first orbital launch from continental European soil**, consolidating Europe's space sovereignty and reducing its dependence on external launchers. The mission, named *"Onward and Upward"*, was aborted at T-3 seconds due to a cause unrelated to the vehicle.

---

## 2. Vehicle: Spectrum Rocket

The **Spectrum** is a small-to-medium-sized two-stage launcher with the following technical characteristics:

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | 28 m |
| Diameter | 2 m |
| Number of Engines | 10 (9 in 1st stage + 1 vacuum in 2nd) |
| Liftoff Thrust | 675 kN |
| Payload to LEO | 1,000 kg (from Kourou) |
| Payload to SSO | 700 kg (from Andøya) |
| Propellants | Propane + Liquid Oxygen (LOX) |

**Aquila Engine:** Isar Aerospace's proprietary design, optimized for propane/LOX. Using propane over kerosene (RP-1) offers higher specific impulse and cleaner combustion. The second stage features **multiple in-flight restart** capability, eliminating the need for a kick stage to reach different target orbits.

**Manufacturing:** The Spectrum is designed and manufactured almost entirely in-house, with vehicles 3 through 7 already in production. A new **40,000 m²** facility near Munich will become operational in 2026.

---

## 3. "Onward and Upward" Mission — Description and Payload

Unlike the first test flight (March 2025, with no operational payload), this mission carried **six real payloads** under the **ESA Boost!** program:

| Payload | Operator |
|---|---|
| CyBEEsat | Technische Universität Berlin |
| TriSat-S | University of Maribor (Slovenia) |
| Platform 6 | EnduroSat |
| FramSat-1 | Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU) |
| SpaceTeamSat1 | TU Wien Space Team (Austria) |
| Let It Go *(non-separable experiment)* | Dcubed (Germany) |

**Target Orbit:** Sun-Synchronous Orbit (SSO), launching in a northwest direction from Andøya.

## 4. Abortion Chronology — Technical Analysis

The abortion was not due to a vehicle failure but rather a chain of operational events:

1. **19:21 UTC** — Original launch window opened. Countdown progressing nominally; all systems verified.
2. **~19:18 UTC** — Isar Aerospace was notified of an **unauthorized fishing vessel intrusion** into the active maritime exclusion zone.
3. **15-minute Hold** — The countdown stopped just before the final automatic sequence. Efforts were made to contact and clear the area.
4. **21:18 UTC** — The zone was declared clear. The countdown resumed.
5. **T-3 seconds** — The automatic launch system **aborted before engine ignition**. Cause: the hold time caused a **temperature increase in the first stage propellant** (propane) that exceeded the Aquila engine's operational margins, making a safe ignition impossible within the remaining window.
6. **Mission cancelled.** No new date announced at the close of this report.

> **Technical Root Cause:** It was not a hardware failure but an indirect consequence of the exclusion zone violation. Propane, being cryogenic, requires a very precise temperature range in the fuel manifold. The ~15-minute delay was enough for the temperature to deviate outside the threshold of the automatic control system.

## 5. Attempt History and Previous Context

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| Mar. 30, 2025 | **First Flight (Going Full Spectrum)** — anomaly ~30 s after liftoff; impact in the ocean. No operational payload. First private European launcher to take off from continental Europe. |
| Jan. 21, 2026 | Second attempt cancelled due to **defective pressurization valve**. |
| Mar. 19, 2026 | Attempt cancelled due to **adverse winds** at Andøya. Rescheduled to March 23. |
| Mar. 23, 2026 | Further delay due to weather conditions. Rescheduled to March 25. |
| **Mar. 25, 2026** | **Abort at T-3 s** due to unauthorized vessel in exclusion zone → propellant temperature increase. |

---

## 6. Strategic Implications and Next Steps

- Isar Aerospace maintains the goal of **European sovereign orbital access before 2027**, a requirement of ESA's **European Launcher Challenge** program.
- The company recently signed a contract to launch Astroscale's **ELSA-M mission**, dedicated to active space debris removal, demonstrating a customer portfolio in high-strategic-value segments.
- The percentage of applications with a **defense mission profile** has increased from 15% to ~60% in the last 12 months, according to CEO Daniel Metzler.
- A **new funding round** is being negotiated that would value the company at over **2 billion euros**.
