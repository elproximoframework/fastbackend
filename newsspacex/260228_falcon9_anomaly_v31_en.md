# Falcon 9 Second Stage Anomaly: Loss of Starlink v3.2 Mission (Flight 31)

SpaceX has confirmed a critical anomaly during the launch of Falcon 9 **Flight 31**, carrying 20 Starlink v3.2 satellites to low Earth orbit. While the first stage completed its ascent and landing successfully, the second stage experienced a liquid oxygen (LOX) leak that prevented the completion of the mission.

## Anatomy of the Failure: LOX Leak and "Hard Start"

Launch telemetry showed the formation of an ice cloud around the **Merlin Vacuum (MVac)** engine shortly after stage separation. The preliminary investigation, overseen by the FAA, points to a **structural crack in a sense line** connected to a pressure sensor in the liquid oxygen system.

This crack, caused by material fatigue from extreme ascent vibrations, allowed liquid oxygen to leak into the engine's thermal insulation. This leak caused excessive cooling of the ignition components, resulting in a "hard start" when the second stage attempted its second burn to circularize the orbit. The engine suffered physical damage and could not complete the maneuver.

## Mission Impact and Satellite Deployment

Despite the engine failure, the second stage managed to deploy the 20 Starlink satellites. However, due to the lack of the second burn, the satellites were released into an extremely low elliptical orbit, with a perigee of just 135 km.

Despite efforts by SpaceX control teams to raise the orbit using the satellites' ion thrusters, atmospheric drag at that altitude was insurmountable. It has been confirmed that all 20 satellites have reentered and disintegrated in Earth's atmosphere.

## FAA Investigation and Operational Pause

Following standard protocol, the **Federal Aviation Administration (FAA)** has initiated a formal investigation into the incident. SpaceX has temporarily halted Falcon 9 launches while auditing all pressure lines across the fleet and implementing corrective measures, which could include redesigning sensor mounts or removing redundant lines.

This marks the first significant in-flight failure for the Falcon 9 in over a decade, breaking an unprecedented streak of consecutive successful launches.
