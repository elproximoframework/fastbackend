# Revolution at Mechazilla: Tower 2 Arms Abandon Hydraulics for Electric Actuators

At the peak of the Starship program, where engineers have spent months preparing Pad 2 for Flight 12, a design decision has been made that might go unnoticed in the headlines but perfectly summarizes SpaceX's philosophy: the capture and stacking arms of **Mechazilla Tower 2** have abandoned their hydraulic actuation system in favor of **fully electric actuators**.

## Why This Change Matters

Hydraulic systems have been the aerospace industry standard for decades for moving massive structures with high force. The reason is simple: pressurized hydraulic oil transmits enormous loads in small spaces. But the Mechazilla arms operate in an environment that no conventional hydraulic system has faced before: in immediate proximity to thousands of tons of **cryogenic liquid oxygen and methane**, two propellants that, in the presence of flammable hydraulic fluid and a spark, produce catastrophic consequences.

By migrating to electric actuators, SpaceX eliminates that fire risk at its root. But the benefits go beyond safety. Electric actuators offer **far superior control precision**: the system can control the position, speed, and force of the arms with a granularity that hydraulic systems simply cannot achieve. This precision is critical when the arms must catch a 200-ton booster descending at controlled but still considerable speeds, or when they must position a 120-ton ship with millimeter tolerance during stacking. Furthermore, electric actuators require **significantly less maintenance**: there are no pressurized fluid lines to check, no seals to wear out, and no possibility of leaks.

## Context: Chopsticks Offline and the Transition to the New Design

This change occurs while Starbase teams work to **reactivate the chopstick trolley system of Tower 1**, which has been out of service for months, forcing technicians to move them manually. Tower 2 was born directly with the electric design, benefiting from everything learned at Tower 1. The architecture of the new ground support equipment (GSE) has also changed: the tanks, residual hydraulic systems, and electrical lines that previously surrounded the OLM have been moved to a **reinforced side bunker**, connecting to the pad only through a single secure central node.

This decision is not just engineering; it is a statement of intent about the future direction of the program. The systems designed today at Pad 2 will be replicated at Starbase's renovated Pad 1, at the new pads in Florida, and potentially, on Mars.
