# Unstoppable Falcon 9: B1076 Breaks World Record with 34th Flight and Transporter-16 Takes Spain to Space

In the last week of March 2026, the Falcon 9 demonstrated once again why it is the most reliable and prolific operational rocket in history with two missions that go beyond technical data: they are milestones of industrial civilization.

## B1076: The Rocket That Has Traveled to Space 34 Times

On March 30, at 5:15 PM EDT, the **B1076** booster lifted off from Cape Canaveral's SLC-40 pad with 29 Starlink satellites on board. Upon returning and landing smoothly on the autonomous drone ship in the Atlantic, B1076 completed its **34th flight**: the absolute record for reuse of an orbital rocket in the history of astronautics. In its 34 trips, this booster has carried cargo to the International Space Station, astronauts on crewed missions, commercial satellites of all kinds, and dozens of Starlink payloads. Each of those flights ends with the same result: the booster lands, is inspected, recertified, and flies again. A cycle that SpaceX has repeated 34 times with the same hardware without a single failure.

To put the magnitude of this achievement into context: when SpaceX was founded in 2002, the industry paradigm was that rockets were disposable by design. B1076 has flown more times than all Saturn V rockets combined.

## Transporter-16: Spain, 119 Payloads, and the Most Powerful Satellite in History

Just twelve hours earlier, on March 30 at 11:02 UTC, another Falcon 9 mission lifted off from Vandenberg Space Force Base: the **Transporter-16** mission, the sixteenth in SpaceX's rideshare launch series. On board, **119 distinct payloads** from dozens of countries, companies, and government agencies, making this flight one of the most heavily loaded rideshare launches in history.

Among the payloads, the presence of **ICEYE**, a synthetic aperture radar company based in Valencia, Spain, stands out as it expands its Earth surveillance constellation. The mission also includes two ICEYE satellites for the Polish military as part of the POLSARIS sovereign observation program. But the star payload is **Gravitas**, from the company **K2 Space**: the company's first "Mega" class satellite, with a wingspan of nearly 40 meters and an electrical power generation capacity of **20 kilowatts**. Partially funded by the U.S. Space Force as a technology demonstration, Gravitas is the most powerful power-generating satellite ever placed in orbit by a private company and could be the precursor to the hardware needed for the orbital data centers that SpaceX has in mind as part of its merger with xAI. K2 Space closed a $250 million funding round in December 2025 to scale production of these large vehicles.
