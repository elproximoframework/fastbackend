# 90-Meter Mega-Doors in Florida and Over 170 Long-Duration Tests at McGregor

The week of March 28 to April 3, 2026, has provided two snapshots of the industrial dimension the Starship ecosystem is reaching: one on the East Coast of Florida, where the Gigabay is growing at a rate that already surpasses its twin in Texas, and another on the plains of McGregor, where Raptor 3 engines are accumulating test hours at a rate unprecedented in the history of space propulsion.

## Florida: The Doors That Confirm Block 4

The Gigabay at **Roberts Road**, in Florida's Kennedy Space Center, is already being clad with its exterior walls while advancing through the fifth and final level of its structure. But the figure that has caught the attention of the engineering community is the height of its main doors: **90 meters of clear opening**. To put this in context: Booster 19, the tallest Super Heavy ever built at 72.3 meters, would fit into this facility with ample room. That extra margin isn't there by accident. With a 90-meter door, SpaceX is implicitly confirming that the Florida Gigabay has been designed to accommodate a future generation of taller boosters—the rumored **Block 4**—without the need for costly structural renovations.

Construction in Florida is moving even faster than the Starbase Gigabay, which remains at level 3 while Florida's has already reached level 4 on its western face. The fact that SpaceX has accelerated construction in Florida reflects the increasing strategic importance of the Atlantic spaceport, where work is also advancing at **LC-39A** and **SLC-37** for the first Starship launches from the East Coast.

## McGregor: 30 Tests in a Few Weeks, Two of 200 Seconds

At the **McGregor, Texas** engine test facility, SpaceX engineers have completed a total of **30 Raptor engine tests** in recent weeks, including two of particularly significant duration: **200 seconds each**. This number is not arbitrary: it is practically the same number of seconds a Super Heavy needs to perform the return boost-back and landing maneuver at the Mechazilla tower after stage separation. In other words, these are tests that simulate the full cycle of the recovery maneuver on the ground.

Since August 2024, SpaceX has already accumulated more than **170 long-duration tests** of Raptor 3 engines at McGregor. To add historical perspective: a camera at the facility recently captured nozzles from an old Raptor 1 being transported through. Seeing a Raptor 1 alongside a Raptor 3 today is like comparing a 1960s carburetor with a modern direct injection system: the technological distance is enormous, and not decades but barely five years have passed between them.
