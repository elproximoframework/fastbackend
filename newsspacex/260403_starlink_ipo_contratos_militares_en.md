# SpaceX Goes Public: The Largest IPO in History, Military Contracts, and Command of Commercial Space

On April 1, 2026, SpaceX confidentially filed its registration statement for an **Initial Public Offering (IPO)** with the US SEC (Securities and Exchange Commission), confirming what the financial sector had been anticipating for months: the largest IPO in history is underway, with a target valuation ranging between **$1.5 and $1.75 trillion** and a fundraising goal of up to **$75 billion**.

## The Largest IPO in History: The Numbers

To put the magnitude of the operation into context: the previous largest IPO in US history was Alibaba's in 2014, which raised $22 billion. SpaceX is seeking more than triple that. The target valuation of $1.75 trillion would place the company above every company in the S&P 500 except for Nvidia, Apple, Alphabet, Microsoft, and Amazon. The Nasdaq listing is planned for **June 2026**, with the public S-1 expected in late April or early May.

The driver of the valuation is not the rockets, but **Starlink**: the satellite internet service closed 2025 with more than 9.2 million subscribers and over $10 billion in annual revenue. Quilty Space analysts project that SpaceX will generate nearly $20 billion in total revenue in 2026, with an EBITDA of $14 billion. An additional element that complicates—or enriches—the financial narrative is the **merger with xAI**, Musk's artificial intelligence company, completed in February 2026, which reframes SpaceX as an integrated launch, connectivity, and orbital computing platform.

## Military Contracts: From the 2014 Lawsuit to 60% Dominance

The other major valuation lever is SpaceX's position in the US military launch market. On April 1, 2026, the **Space Force** awarded SpaceX a **$178.5 million** contract to launch missile-tracking satellites for the Space Development Agency on two Falcon 9 missions starting in the third quarter of 2027. This contract is the latest piece of a dominance that SpaceX has built from scratch: in 2014, the company had to sue the Air Force even to be allowed to compete against the United Launch Alliance (ULA) monopoly. Twelve years later, SpaceX controls approximately **60% of United States military launches** through 2032 under the National Security Space Launch (NSSL) Phase 3 program, having been awarded a $5.9 billion framework contract in April 2025 for 28 national security missions through 2029.

Since 2008, SpaceX has accumulated more than **$24.4 billion in contracts with the federal government** (NASA, Air Force, Space Force), making it practically indispensable for US space policy.
