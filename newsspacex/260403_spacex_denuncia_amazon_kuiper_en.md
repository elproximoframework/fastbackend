# SpaceX Files Complaint Against Amazon with the FCC: 30 Evasion Maneuvers in a Few Hours and an Orbital War

On April 1, 2026, SpaceX filed a formal complaint with the **Federal Communications Commission (FCC)** against **Amazon** and its satellite internet constellation, officially known as **Amazon Leo** (formerly Project Kuiper). The complaint opens one of the most complex fronts in the space industry: the coexistence of megaconstelations in an increasingly crowded low Earth orbit.

## The Core of the Complaint: Unauthorized Altitudes

SpaceX alleges that Amazon and its launch partner **Arianespace** have systematically failed to comply with the orbital debris mitigation plan approved by the FCC, deploying their satellites at insertion orbits **50 to 90 kilometers higher** than authorized—which was "near 400 km"—in eight different launches, without submitting an amended plan or requesting the Commission's approval for that change.

The most serious case occurred on **February 12, 2026**, when Arianespace's Ariane 6 launched a batch of Amazon Leo satellites at an altitude high enough to create collision risks that SpaceX described as "impossible to mitigate" with dozens of its operational satellites. The immediate consequence: Starlink satellites had to execute **30 emergency evasion maneuvers** in a few hours. "Amazon and Arianespace's negligence unnecessarily and significantly increases the risk to other operating systems and crewed spacecraft," the complaint concludes.

## The Context That Complicates the Story

The irony of the matter is considerable. Amazon revealed in a response filed with the FCC on April 2 that SpaceX launched **three batches of Amazon Leo satellites** on its own Falcon 9 rockets in 2025—at altitudes of 460 km—without raising any objections at that time. Amazon argues that SpaceX only began complaining "in recent months," coinciding with SpaceX's own decision to lower thousands of its Starlink satellites from 550 km to 475-485 km, which brought them closer to Amazon's insertion altitudes.

Amazon, for its part, insists that its license does not require an exact deployment at 400 km but rather that it has "some operational flexibility," and that it is already working with Arianespace to modify insertion plans to a lower target on the fourth Ariane 6 launch. Jeff Bezos's company also revealed that it has purchased **10 additional Falcon 9 launches** at the end of 2025 to accelerate the deployment of its constellation, with a goal of 20 annual launches carrying up to 48 satellites per mission.

The regulatory clash comes at a time when Amazon Leo has only about 200 satellites in orbit compared to Starlink's more than 10,000 active ones, but it is accelerating its deployment so as not to miss the market window. The FCC must decide whether there is a formal license violation or if, as Amazon maintains, the situation is the result of operational decisions made by both parties legitimately.
