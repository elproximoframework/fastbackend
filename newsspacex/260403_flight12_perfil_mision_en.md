# Flight 12 Profile: Integrated Hot Staging, Ocean Splashdown, and Active Payload in Orbit

**Starship Flight 12** is not a repeat mission. Although the general profile—launch, stage separation, suborbital flight, ocean splashdown for both vehicles—bears similarities to the latest Block 2 flights, the hardware driving that script has changed radically. And the payload aboard Ship 39 introduces a novelty that could have far-reaching implications for the future of the program.

## Integrated Hot Staging: Goodbye to the Separate Ring

One of the most visible modifications of Booster 19 compared to its predecessors is the **integration of the hot staging mechanism directly into the booster's structure**, eliminating the separate ring used in previous generations. Hot staging is the technique where the second-stage engines (the ship) ignite while still attached to the booster, generating additional thrust that cleanly separates the vehicles before the booster begins its return maneuver. This integration makes the assembly lighter and simplifies the separation operation. Furthermore, in Flight 12, Booster 19 will not attempt to be caught by the Mechazilla arms at Pad 2; it will splash down in the ocean. SpaceX has decided to be cautious in the first flight of the new generation, prioritizing performance data collection over recovery demonstration.

## A Payload That Lives in Orbit for One Hour

The most intriguing element of Flight 12 is the nature of the cargo. Thanks to an **FCC license update that took effect on April 7, 2026**, it is known that SpaceX will perform a suborbital payload deployment. But these will not be the usual inert mass blocks. The mass simulators for Flight 12 will be **functionally active**: equipped with cameras, antennas, and the ability to connect to the Starlink constellation for approximately one hour before re-entering and disintegrating in the atmosphere.

Speculation in the community is intense. The two most plausible options are: early prototypes of Starlink V3 satellites in a reduced configuration, or hardware related to the orbital data center projects that SpaceX has been developing for some time. The combined thrust of Ship 39's six **RVac (Raptor Vacuum)** engines exceeds **1,700 metric tons**, a figure that, as the second stage of a rocket, is unparalleled in the entire history of astronautics. No operational rocket—except for the Super Heavy itself—matches or exceeds the power of the Starship spacecraft.

## The New Generation: More Payload, More Capacity

Flight 12 inaugurates the **Starship V3** architecture, which according to SpaceX projections is capable of carrying more than **100 metric tons to low Earth orbit** in a reusable configuration, compared to approximately 35 tons for Starship Block 2. A threefold jump in capacity in a single version of the vehicle. This increase, combined with the launch from Pad 2 (the first in the program's history from that platform), makes Flight 12 the Starship flight with the highest density of "firsts" since the first integrated flight in April 2023.
