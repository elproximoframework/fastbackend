# Booster 19 and the 33 Raptor 3s: The Most Powerful Rocket in History Comes to Life

Inside **Starbase's Mega Bay 1**, a team of SpaceX engineers is working against the clock to complete the installation of the thirty-third engine for Booster 19. This booster, designated as the first **Super Heavy Block 3**, is the candidate for Flight 12 of the Starship program and the vehicle that will hold the title of the most powerful flying object ever assembled.

## A Booster Built in Record Time

Booster 19 has an origin story marked by adversity. Its predecessor, Booster 18, suffered a catastrophic liquid oxygen tank rupture during a ground test on November 21, 2025, and had to be scrapped. SpaceX began stacking Booster 19 just six days later, on November 27, and completed the full structure on December 24: **26 days of construction**, half the time it took for Booster 17. A record assembly speed that reflects the industrial maturity the program is achieving.

After passing its cryogenic tests at the Massey's site during the first week of February 2026, Booster 19 was moved to Mega Bay 1 for the installation of its 33 **Raptor 3** engines, SpaceX's third-generation propulsion system. The process is radically different from previous generations: while in early prototypes teams could install 29 engines in a single night, now each unit requires between three and ten days of meticulous work. Each engine must be aligned with millimeter precision, with all oxygen, methane, electrical systems, and gimbal actuator lines individually verified.

## The Raptor 3: A Generational Leap

The **Raptor 3** represents the absolute state of the art in chemical propulsion. With an approximate thrust of 280 metric tons per unit—22% more than Raptor 2—and a thrust-to-weight ratio exceeding 200, it is the highest-performing rocket engine ever mass-produced. SpaceX has accumulated more than **40,000 seconds of test time** on the Raptor 3 at its McGregor, Texas facility before installing it for the first time on a flight vehicle. Its simplified design eliminates hydraulic systems for thrust control units, replacing them with electric actuators, which reduces potential failure points and increases the overall energy efficiency of the assembly.

With all 33 engines fully installed, Booster 19 will generate a **combined liftoff thrust of approximately 9,240 metric tons**, far exceeding the thrust of the Saturn V (3,400 tons) or the SLS Block 1 (3,988 tons). No other flying object in human history exists with such propulsion capability.

## Tests at Pad 2 and Next Steps

Booster 19 was rolled out to Starbase's **Pad 2** on March 8, 2026, starting a test campaign on the new pad that includes pressurization tests, cryogenic loading, and spin primes. On March 16, the first static fire with 10 Raptor 3 engines was performed, which was prematurely interrupted by a ground support system (GSE) issue, although all ignited engines performed correctly. After rollback to install the remaining 23 engines, the next critical milestone is the **full 33-engine static fire**, the result of which will directly determine the Flight 12 date. The synchronization of the opening of the 20 OLM hold-down arms—satisfactorily tested in full sequence for the first time on the night of March 27—is one of the clearest indicators that this test is imminent.
