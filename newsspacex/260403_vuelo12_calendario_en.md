# Estimated Schedule for Flight 12: Five Milestones to Watch Before Liftoff

Elon Musk confirmed on April 3, 2026, on his social network X that the next Starship flight—the debut of the V3 architecture—is **between 4 and 6 weeks away**, placing the launch window between late April and mid-May. However, that estimate is contingent on the success of a specific chain of technical milestones, none of which is trivial.

## The Five Critical Milestones Before Liftoff

The first milestone—and the most urgent—is the **Ship 39 static fire at Massey's**. The FAA has established an air restriction over the site until April 14, defining the most likely window for this test. Ship 39 will need to ignite its six Raptor Vacuum engines simultaneously, validating the full propulsion system before flight. This is the first time a Block 3 spacecraft will perform a full static fire.

The second milestone is the **33-engine static fire of Booster 19** at Pad 2. With the installation of the 23 remaining engines underway in Mega Bay 1, the booster must return to the pad, pass pressurization and LOX checks, and demonstrate that the 33 Raptor 3 engines work in coordination without anomalies. This is by far the most uncertain milestone on the schedule: a single engine anomaly can force an additional rollback for inspection.

The third milestone is the **stacking of the full assembly** (Ship 39 on Booster 19) at Pad 2. This operation, already demonstrated multiple times at Pad 1, will be performed for the first time on the new platform with Block 3 hardware.

The fourth milestone is the **decision on Booster 20**: although it won't fly on this mission, the status of the Super Crusher structural tests at Massey's could determine if it requires additional reinforcements before being sealed, which would affect the program's cadence beyond Flight 12.

The fifth and decisive milestone is **launch authorization from the FAA**. The regulatory agency has historically been the most unpredictable variable in Starship schedules. The FCC communications license has already been granted for windows between May 5 and 10, but the FAA launch license is a separate and more complex process. If all static fires occur without incident between April 3 and 21, and the FAA acts with agility, a launch in late April or early May is achievable. If any test generates data requiring additional analysis, the schedule would extend to mid-May or beyond.
