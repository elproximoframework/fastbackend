# Anomaly in Starlink-34343 and the Milestone of 10,000 active satellites in orbit

On March 29, 2026, at an altitude of about 560 kilometers above Earth, the **Starlink-34343** satellite stopped responding. What SpaceX initially described as an "on-orbit anomaly resulting in loss of communications" was confirmed shortly thereafter by the space tracking company **LeoLabs** as a fragmentation event: its radars detected "dozens of objects" in the immediate vicinity of the satellite following the incident.

## What we know about the incident

LeoLabs characterized the event as caused by "an internal energy source, not by a collision with space debris or another satellite." The technical expression points to propulsion systems—the satellite's argon ion thrusters—or batteries as the most likely causes of the event. Starlink-34343 was a **V2 Mini Optimized** satellite only ten months old, launched on May 27, 2025, from Vandenberg aboard a Falcon 9 as part of the constellation's Group 17-1.

The incident is particularly concerning because it is not the first. On December 17, 2025, Starlink-35956 suffered a seemingly similar anomaly that caused its propellant tank to empty, a 4 km drop in altitude, and the generation of a small cloud of fragments. The analogy LeoLabs draws between the two events points to a possible **shared design flaw** in a subset of the fleet, although SpaceX has not confirmed if they share a root cause. The company reacted by launching 29 additional satellites just six hours after the March incident, without signs of an operational pause like the one that occurred after the December event.

The good news is that the low altitude of the event ensures that the fragments will deorbit and burn up in the atmosphere within weeks. Neither the ISS, nor the Artemis II mission, nor Transporter-16 were at risk.

## The milestone of 10,000 active satellites

This incident occurs, somewhat ironically, as Starlink has just passed the symbolic barrier of **10,000 active satellites in orbit**. According to data from the tracker KeepTrack, as of March 23, 2026, SpaceX maintained 11,641 satellites launched, 10,126 in orbit, and 10,116 functionally active. This figure represents approximately **two out of every three active satellites in Earth orbit**, from any operator and any country. An unprecedented historical concentration of space assets.

The road here took twelve years to complete since the first experimental satellite and four years since the first operational launch in 2020. The future is even more ambitious: SpaceX has regulatory approval for tens of thousands of additional satellites and is studying satellite architectures as orbital data center nodes.
